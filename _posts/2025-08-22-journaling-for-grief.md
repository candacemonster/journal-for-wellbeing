Some of the most important things we carry have no polite vocabulary. Grief is one of them.

The condolence cards run out. People stop asking. But on a Tuesday morning for no obvious reason, the loss sits right there at the breakfast table, as present as it was the first week. Journaling can be one of the few places where grief has permission to take up exactly as much space as it actually needs.

This guide is for anyone sitting with loss, whether recent or old, visible or invisible, and wondering if the page might help.

---

### What Research Says About Writing and Grief

The body of research on expressive writing and grief is genuinely encouraging, though nuanced enough to be worth explaining honestly.

Psychologist James Pennebaker's foundational research on expressive writing found that writing about deeply emotional events, including losses, was associated with improved long-term mood, better immune functioning, and reduced rumination. More recent research has examined grief specifically. Studies have found that structured expressive writing for bereaved individuals can reduce psychological distress, particularly intrusive thoughts and avoidance, and help people begin to make meaning of their loss over time.

Meaning-making is central here. Grief researchers like Robert Neimeyer emphasise that one of the core tasks of mourning is reconstructing a sense of meaning and identity after a loss. Journaling can support this by helping you:

- Give your grief language, because unnamed feelings tend to grow larger
- Notice how your grief is changing, even if it does not feel linear
- Hold contradictory emotions at once (relief and sadness, love and anger)
- Begin to reconnect with who you are now, after the loss

At the same time, journaling is not a framework for "getting through" grief on a schedule. There is no timeline, no correct amount of pages. And for acute or complicated grief, especially where there are thoughts of self-harm or an inability to function, professional support, grief counselling or therapy, is important, and the page alone is not enough.

### Grief That Doesn't Have a Name

Not all grief looks like bereavement. Many people carry forms of loss that do not receive the same social acknowledgement:

- The end of a relationship, friendship, or community
- A miscarriage or fertility loss
- Grieving someone who is still alive (estrangement, dementia, addiction)
- The loss of a previous version of yourself, through illness, disability, or life change
- Grieving a life you thought you would have that did not happen

These losses are real. They deserve the same gentleness on the page.

### Low-Pressure Ways to Grieve Through Writing

You do not have to write about the loss directly. Sometimes beginning anywhere is more important than beginning with the hardest thing.

**The grief log**
Once a day, or once a week, write one sentence that is honest about where you are: *"Still heavy"* or *"Lighter today, and guilty about that"* or *"I don't feel anything right now and that scares me."* A grief log is not a processing exercise. It is a record that you are moving through time.

**Letters you never send**
Write to the person you lost, to the version of yourself before the loss, to the relationship, or to the grief itself. You do not have to send them. The act of addressing something directly can reach places that reflective journaling cannot.

**One memory, in detail**
Choose one specific memory and describe it as though you are watching it through a window: the room, the light, what was said, what you noticed. Writing memory in detail can honour what was real without requiring you to say anything about your feelings.

**The things I'm carrying list**
Write a list of everything you are currently holding: the logistics, the feelings, the unanswered questions, the things people don't know. You are not trying to solve any of it. You are just setting it where you can see it.

### Before You Write: A Moment to Arrive

Grief can make the nervous system feel fragile. Before a journaling session, especially if the material is deep, take one to two minutes:

- Place one hand on your chest or your heart.
- Take three slow breaths, just slightly longer on the exhale.
- Say to yourself, aloud or silently: *"I have some time for this. I can stop whenever I need to."*

After you write, do something small and concrete: wash your hands, make a drink, step outside. This signals to your body that the session has a boundary.

### 18 Journal Prompts for Grief

These prompts are designed to offer entry points rather than endpoints. Answer any one of them slowly, in your own time.

**For the loss itself**
1. *"The thing I miss most that no one else might think of is..."*
2. *"What I wish I had said, or said more, was..."*
3. *"The version of me that existed before this loss used to..."*
4. *"Something that has surprised me about my grief is..."*
5. *"A way I have changed since the loss that I am still getting to know is..."*

**For the hard parts of grief**
6. *"The feeling I have been most afraid to write down is... and I wonder if I could give it just two sentences here."*
7. *"The story about the loss that I keep replaying is... and one alternative interpretation I haven't let myself consider is..."*
8. *"Something I feel guilty about in relation to the loss is... and what I might say to a friend who felt this same guilt is..."*
9. *"Alongside grief, I have also felt... and I am learning to let both be true."*
10. *"A way I have been hard on myself about how I am grieving is..."*

**For invisible grief**
11. *"The loss that doesn't have a name, or that others don't seem to understand, is..."*
12. *"What I grieve about the relationship or the version of life I lost is..."*
13. *"The part of grieving this in private that has been the hardest is..."*

**For memory and honouring**
14. *"A way this person, place, or chapter shaped who I am is..."*
15. *"A small ritual or act that feels like honouring the loss is..."*
16. *"One memory I am afraid I will forget is... and I want to record it here."*

**For the present and forward**
17. *"Something that has held me, even a little, during this grief is..."*
18. *"A version of carrying this loss gently, without it crushing me, might look like..."*

### What To Do If Writing Feels Like Too Much

On some days, grief makes the page feel impossible or cruel. On those days, you do not have to write. You can:

- Read back through old entries, just to witness how far you have come or how long you have carried this
- Write someone else's words, a poem, a lyric, a passage that says something you feel, and leave it on the page without adding anything
- Draw a line, mark the date, and put the notebook down

Coming back to the page another day is not failure. It is the nature of grief, which moves in tides, not in straight lines.

---

### When to Seek Support

If your grief is accompanied by persistent inability to function, thoughts of harming yourself, or a sense of being stuck in the same intense place after many months, please reach out to a grief counsellor, therapist, or your doctor. Complicated grief is a real clinical phenomenon with effective treatments, and getting help is not a sign that you are grieving wrong. It is a sign that this loss was significant, and that you deserve more than you can offer yourself alone.

You are allowed to need more than a page. The page can still be part of it.
