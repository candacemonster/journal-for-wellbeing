A wellness journal does not have to be a perfectly curated planner. It can be a simple place where you track what actually affects your mood, energy, and nervous system, so you can take kinder, more informed care of yourself.

---

### Why Tracking Can Help Your Mental Health

Tracking a few key areas over time can help you:

- Notice patterns you might miss day to day
- See how sleep, stressors, and small joys relate to your mood
- Catch early signs of burnout or emotional overload

The key is focusing on meaningful, low-pressure data rather than "perfect" routines.

---

### Idea 1: Mood and Energy Check Ins

Each day, jot:

- **Mood:** 1–3 words (for example, "anxious," "steady," "flat")
- **Energy:** low, medium, or high

You can add a simple 1–10 scale if you like, but even basic labels help you see how your emotional and physical energy rise and fall over weeks.

---

### Idea 2: Sleep Quality and Rest

Instead of tracking exact hours, you can note:

- "Slept: poor, okay, good"
- "Rested: yes / no / a little"

Both sleep quality and perceived rest strongly influence mood and stress tolerance.

---

### Idea 3: Stressors and Supports

On each day's page, include two tiny sections:

- **"Stressors today:"** 1–2 quick words (for example, "work," "family conflict," "money")
- **"Supports today:"** 1–2 quick words (for example, "walk," "friend," "therapist," "music")

Seeing both side by side can help you remember that even hard days often include some support, and can highlight what helps you cope.

---

### Idea 4: Tiny Joys or "Glimmers"

Glimmers are small moments that feel okay or pleasant, even in difficult seasons. You might add a daily line:

**"One small good thing:"**

Examples: "sun on the kitchen table," "cat curled up next to me," "song I liked in the car." This is not forced positivity, it is nervous system support through noticing safety and warmth.

---

### Idea 5: Body Signals

You can track one or two body signals that matter for you:

- Tension (for example, "shoulders tight," "stomach knot," or "looser today")
- Pain or discomfort
- Appetite (for example, "normal," "low," "high")

Physical signals often show stress before thoughts do, so noticing them can help you intervene earlier.

---

### Idea 6: Coping Tools Used

Add a small section that says:

**"What helped today:"**

List one or two coping tools you used, such as "walked outside," "messaged a friend," "deep breaths," "turned off notifications," or "therapy session." Over time, this becomes a personalized menu of what actually supports you.

---

### Idea 7: Boundaries and "No's"

Once or twice a week, you might reflect on:

**"A boundary I held or wish I had held:"**

This helps you notice where you are honoring or overriding your limits, which is central to emotional wellbeing.

---

### Idea 8: Values and Alignment

You can occasionally add:

**"Something I did that aligned with my values today:"**

For example, "showed up for a friend," "rested when I was tired," or "created something." Values-based tracking can increase a sense of meaning, even in hard seasons.

---

### Keep It Light and Humane

You do not need to track everything every day. The point is not to optimize your life into perfection, it is to see yourself more clearly so you can respond with a little more wisdom and care.
