A therapy journal can be a powerful bridge between sessions. It helps you remember insights, track patterns, and arrive more prepared. The key is to use it as a support, not as a place to overprocess or do therapy alone.

## Why a therapy journal can help

Therapists and mental health organizations often recommend journaling between sessions to:

- Capture thoughts and feelings while they are fresh
- Notice patterns in triggers, moods, and coping
- Track progress over time, especially on rough days
- Gather questions or events you want to bring into therapy

Studies and clinical guidance note that journaling between sessions can deepen insight and make your time in therapy more effective, as long as it feels manageable.

## What a therapy journal is (and is not)

**A therapy journal is:**

- A companion to therapy, not a replacement
- A place to jot key moments, feelings, and questions
- A tool to help you remember and reflect

**It is not:**

- A full transcript of sessions
- A place where you must "do the work" perfectly
- A pressure to prove you are trying hard enough

Keeping this frame can prevent the journal from becoming another source of stress.

## A simple structure for after each session

Within 24 hours of a session, take 5–10 minutes and answer a few of these:

- "One thing that stood out to me today was…"
- "A feeling that came up in session that surprised me was…"
- "One idea or tool I want to remember is…"
- "Something I might want to explore more next time is…"

Therapists who share therapy journal templates often encourage brief, focused reflections like this instead of long, exhaustive recaps.

## Daily or weekly check-ins between sessions

On days you do not have therapy, you can use your journal to stay connected to the work without overdoing it. Try a short check-in:

- "Today I noticed…" (a trigger, a pattern, a coping win)
- "My mood today was…" (and why, if you know)
- "Something I might want to tell my therapist about is…"

Even a few lines per day about feelings and triggers can make it easier to share meaningful details in your next session.

## What to write down (and what to save for therapy)

**Helpful to include:**

- Moments that felt emotionally big (good or hard)
- Times you used a skill from therapy
- Recurring thoughts, dreams, or fears
- Questions you want to ask

**Better to save for therapy or process together:**

- Very detailed trauma narratives
- Intensely self-blaming or hopeless thoughts that escalate as you write
- Complex decisions you feel unable to hold alone

If you notice yourself getting flooded or spiralling while journaling, pause and simply jot: *"This feels too big to untangle alone. I want to bring it to therapy."* That sentence is often enough.

## How to avoid overprocessing

To keep your therapy journal from becoming a second therapy session that drains you:

- **Set time limits**, for example, 10 minutes after a session, 5 minutes on other days.
- **Use prompts that include boundaries**, such as "What I want to remember, not everything that happened."
- **Close each entry with a settling sentence** like, "I do not have to figure this out tonight. I can bring it to my therapist."

Clinicians who write about therapy journaling emphasize that it should support integration, not recreate the emotional intensity of the session on your own.

## A sample therapy journaling template

You can copy and reuse this for each session:

**After a session:**

- Date:
- Session highlight:
- Feelings I noticed:
- One thing I learned or saw differently:
- One question I might want to ask next time:
- Between now and the next session, I want to gently pay attention to:

**Between sessions:**

- Today's date:
- Mood (1–2 words):
- A moment that felt important:
- Something I might want to share in therapy:

---

Small, consistent notes like these can deepen the work in therapy without overwhelming you. You are not trying to do it all alone, you are just keeping a thread so you can find your way back.
