Journaling is often recommended for anxiety, but for some people and in some situations, it can actually spike distress rather than ease it. This does not mean journaling is wrong for you. It usually means the way you are currently approaching it needs adjustment.

---

### When and Why Journaling Can Increase Anxiety

Research and clinical experience suggest that journaling can temporarily increase uncomfortable feelings when it involves writing about very painful or traumatic experiences, especially without adequate coping skills or emotional support in place. Some people also find that free writing about worries without any structure or grounding can turn into rumination, where thoughts loop and intensify rather than settle.

If you have a tendency toward catastrophic thinking, perfectionism, or harsh self-criticism, unstructured journaling can sometimes amplify those patterns instead of softening them. Similarly, if you journal late at night about distressing topics and then try to sleep immediately, the heightened arousal can interfere with rest.

---

### Signs Journaling Is Spiking Your Anxiety

You might notice that after writing, you feel more jittery, spacey, panicky, or stuck in your head than before you started. Your heart rate might increase, your thoughts might race faster, or you might feel flooded with emotion in a way that does not ease over the next hour or two. If this happens regularly, it is worth changing your approach rather than pushing through.

---

### Adjustment 1: Set a Time Limit Before You Start

Decide how long you will write, for example 5 or 10 minutes, and stop when the timer rings even if you are mid-thought. This creates a clear boundary and prevents journaling from becoming an hours-long spiral. Knowing there is a limit can also make it easier to start, because you are not committing to solving everything in one sitting.

---

### Adjustment 2: Use Grounding Before and After Writing

Spend 1 to 2 minutes before you open your journal doing something that helps you feel anchored in your body, such as noticing your breath, feeling your feet on the floor, or naming five things you can see in the room. After you finish writing, repeat the grounding practice so your nervous system knows the session is closed and it is time to come back to the present moment.

---

### Adjustment 3: Stay Focused on the Present or Recent Past

Instead of diving into old memories or hypothetical future disasters, keep your entries focused on today or this week. You might write about what you are feeling right now, what triggered it, and what might help in the next 24 hours. This narrows the scope and reduces the chance of getting lost in overwhelming material.

---

### Adjustment 4: Add Structure with Prompts

Instead of free writing, use specific prompts that include a grounding or balancing element, such as "What I am worried about right now is…, and one thing I know to be true is…," or "The loudest fear in my mind is…, and one small thing I can do to support myself today is…" Structure helps keep you from spiraling while still giving space to name what is real.

---

### Adjustment 5: Close Each Entry with One Kind Sentence

Before you finish, write one sentence that offers yourself a bit of softness or perspective, such as "I am doing my best with what I have right now," "This feeling is hard, and it will shift," or "I am allowed to put this down and return to it later if I need to." This small ritual can signal closure and prevent you from carrying the intensity forward into the rest of your day.

---

### Adjustment 6: Journal Earlier in the Day, Not Right Before Bed

If nighttime journaling leaves you wired or anxious, try moving your practice to the morning or afternoon when you have more time to process and shift your state before sleep. Some people find that morning pages help clear worry before the day begins, while evening entries can stir things up right when the body is trying to wind down.

---

### Adjustment 7: Pair Journaling with Another Coping Tool

If journaling alone feels too activating, you might combine it with a walk, a short body scan, a few minutes of slow breathing, or a grounding meditation right after. This helps your nervous system complete the cycle of noticing, expressing, and then settling.

---

### When to Pause or Get Support

If you have tried these adjustments and journaling still consistently makes your anxiety worse, it is okay to take a break or work with a therapist to develop a safer approach. Journaling is not the only path to self-awareness, and it is not a failure to recognize that a particular practice is not serving you right now.

For some people, especially those with unprocessed trauma or severe anxiety, journaling is most helpful when it is shaped and supported by a mental health professional who can help you titrate the depth and intensity of what you explore on the page.

Journaling should ultimately help you feel a bit more grounded, clear, or compassionate toward yourself, even if it surfaces uncomfortable feelings along the way. If it is not doing that, you deserve to adjust or replace it with something that does.
