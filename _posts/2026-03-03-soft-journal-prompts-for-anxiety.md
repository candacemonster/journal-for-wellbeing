When anxiety is loud and you haven't journaled in a while, starting again can feel strangely heavy. This list is meant to be a gentle bridge back: prompts you can answer in just a few lines, without fixing everything at once.

You can move through these in order, choose one at random, or circle the ones that feel kindest right now.

---

### How to Use These Prompts (Without Overwhelming Yourself)

Pick just one prompt per sitting, and give yourself permission to stop after 5–10 minutes or even 3–5 sentences.

Before you begin, take three unhurried breaths and notice one point of contact (feet, back, hand on your chest) so your body knows you're here.

If a prompt feels too sharp, skip it. There is no extra credit for pushing yourself into overwhelm.

Close with one kind sentence to yourself, even if it feels small or awkward.

---

**1. "Right now, I feel…"**
Complete the sentence and name whatever is actually there: anxious, numb, buzzy, flat, tangled. You're not trying to choose the "right" emotion; you're just letting your inner weather have a name.

**2. "My anxiety today sounds like…"**
Imagine your anxious thoughts as a voice and describe how it sounds. Is it fast, scolding, panicky, persuasive, tired? Giving it a tone can help you see it as a voice, not the truth.

**3. "The loudest worry in my mind is…"**
Let yourself name the main worry, even if it feels repetitive or embarrassing. Often, writing it down shows you that it's one specific fear, not a vague, unstoppable cloud.

**4. "In my body, anxiety feels like…"**
Describe the sensations: tightness, buzzing, a knot, heaviness, emptiness. Noticing your body's language can help you catch anxiety earlier next time.

**5. "If my anxiety could finish this sentence…"**
*"If my anxiety could finish this sentence, it would say: 'I am trying to protect you from…'"* Let the anxious part answer honestly. Sometimes, writing its intention, even if it is overprotective, softens how you relate to it.

**6. "Right now, I'm afraid that…"**
Name one specific fear and then, in a second sentence, write: *"Another possible outcome I usually don't consider is…"* You're not forcing optimism, just opening a small window to alternatives.

**7. "Today, '10% less pressure' would look like…"**
Describe what 10% less pressure on yourself would mean in concrete terms: fewer tasks, less perfection, more pauses. Tiny reductions in pressure can be more realistic than "no anxiety at all."

**8. "If I spoke to myself like a friend…"**
Write what you would say if someone you loved felt exactly how you feel now. Often, this voice is gentler and more balanced than the one you usually use with yourself.

**9. "One thing I can postpone for 24 hours is…"**
Choose a worry, decision, or task you can consciously delay as an experiment. Anxiety often insists everything is urgent; postponing one thing can create a bit of space.

**10. "Three things that helped me in the past…"**
List three small things that have helped you feel a tiny bit steadier before: going outside, texting a friend, breathing exercises, a certain song. You're reminding yourself that you have history with surviving anxious days.

**11. "If my nervous system could ask for something right now…"**
Write as if your body could speak directly: more water, fewer notifications, a nap, a walk, less multitasking, a real meal. You don't have to grant everything it asks for, but maybe you can choose one.

**12. "What I'm avoiding because I feel anxious is…"**
Name something you've been putting off because anxiety is high. Then add a second sentence: *"The smallest version of this I might be able to try this week is…"*

**13. "One tiny thing I can control today is…"**
Anxiety loves to focus on everything you can't control. This prompt steers your attention toward a single, manageable area of influence: your bedtime, your phone use, your self-talk, your food, your environment.

**14. "Right now, my world feels small/big because…"**
Explore whether things feel too big (overwhelming) or too small (constricted, stuck), and why. Naming the shape of your experience can clarify what kind of support might help.

**15. "If I could ask for support with one specific thing…"**
Write the request you wish you could make to a person, to your future self, or to something larger than you. Clarity about what you need can be powerful, even before you say it out loud.

**16. "When I look back, I can see that I've already survived…"**
List a few moments (big or small) that were hard and you moved through anyway. This is not about minimizing today's anxiety, just reminding yourself that you have evidence of your own resilience.

**17. "What would be 'enough' for today?"**
Instead of a perfect day, describe an "enough" day, a day that would be good enough under the circumstances. This can gently lower the bar that anxiety often sets unreachably high.

**18. "If I could press pause on one expectation…"**
Write about one expectation (from yourself or others) that feels especially heavy. What might shift in your body or day if it paused, even temporarily?

**19. "When I feel like this, I tend to…"**
Notice your patterns: overworking, scrolling, people-pleasing, withdrawing. Seeing the pattern on the page can open room for experimenting with different responses.

**20. "One small comfort I can offer myself is…"**
Describe a comfort that doesn't have to fix everything: a warm drink, a shower, changing clothes, soft music, stepping outside. Comfort is not a luxury; it's a way of signaling safety to your nervous system.

**21. "I notice my all-or-nothing thoughts when I think…"**
Write down a few all-or-nothing sentences your anxious mind uses ("I always mess this up," "If this goes wrong, everything is ruined"), then gently rephrase one of them in more balanced language.

**22. "If I gave myself permission to not fix everything today…"**
Finish the sentence: *"If I gave myself permission to not fix everything today, I might…"* See what softer options appear when you release the pressure to solve your whole life in one afternoon.

**23. "What feels even slightly steady right now is…"**
It might be very small: your chair, your pet, a routine, a person, your ability to breathe. Naming any small stability can anchor you when the rest feels uncertain.

**24. "If I zoomed out and looked at this week as a whole…"**
Write about how you might see this week if you were looking at it from a distance, like chapters in a story rather than the moment you're in right now. Zooming out can gently loosen the grip of today's intensity.

**25. "One sentence I want to carry with me is…"**
Choose or write a sentence you want to remember, a phrase that feels grounding, honest, and kind. It might be *"I'm allowed to go slowly,"* *"I can come back to this later,"* or *"Feeling anxious doesn't mean I'm failing."*

---

### A Gentle Reminder as You Return to the Page

If you've gone days or weeks without journaling, you have not ruined anything. It is normal for practices to come and go, especially when you're living with anxiety. Each time you return, even for just a few lines, you are practicing meeting yourself with a little more honesty and care.
