All or nothing thinking says "I am either doing great or failing completely." It shows up as always, never, ruined, perfect, and hopeless, and it can make your inner world feel very small and harsh. Your journal can be a quiet place to practice more flexible, both–and thinking instead.

## How all or nothing thinking shows up on the page

In your journal, all or nothing thinking might sound like:

- "I always mess this up."
- "If I cannot do it perfectly, there is no point."
- "Today was a disaster."
- "Everyone else is handling life better than me."

Cognitive therapists call this a distortion because almost nothing in real life is truly all good or all bad. Most days are mixed, most people are mixed, and most efforts are partly successful, partly messy.

## Step 1: Notice absolute words

When you write about a situation, underline or highlight words like:

- always, never, everyone, no one
- ruined, perfect, failure, hopeless

Seeing these on the page helps you catch when your mind has slid into extremes.

You can then write a short question under the sentence, such as:

- "Is it literally always like this?"
- "Has there ever been an exception?"

You are not forcing optimism, just gently checking accuracy.

## Step 2: Ask "What is the middle ground?"

Pick one all or nothing sentence and write it at the top of a fresh page. Underneath, answer:

**"What might the middle ground be here?"**

For example, instead of:

*"I completely failed at this project,"*

You might write:

*"Some parts went poorly, and some parts went okay, and I learned things I can use next time."*

This echoes CBT prompts that invite you to look for partial successes and middle ground, which are linked with more balanced emotions.

## Step 3: Write two truths about the same situation

All or nothing thinking says "either this or that," but real life is often "this and that." To practice this in your journal, write:

- "One thing that is hard about this is…"
- "One thing that is still okay or hopeful is…"

For example:

- "One thing that is hard about this is that I missed a deadline."
- "One thing that is still okay is that I communicated with my team and we adjusted."

Holding two truths at once builds emotional flexibility instead of collapse.

## Step 4: Challenge "always/never" with evidence

Choose one strong statement like "I always ruin relationships" or "I never finish anything." Under it, create two small lists:

- **"Times that seem to support this:"** (maybe one or two examples)
- **"Times that do not fit this story:"** (even one small exception counts)

CBT-style questioning uses this kind of evidence check to soften rigid beliefs. You are not pretending the pain is not real, you are reminding your brain that the story is incomplete.

## Step 5: Write how you would talk to a friend

Take a situation where you are being very hard on yourself and write a short paragraph from the perspective of talking to a friend in the same position.

For example:

*"You are not a total failure because this was hard. You were under a lot of pressure, and you still showed up. There were things you could not control, and you are allowed to learn rather than punish yourself."*

This uses the well-known "what would I say to a friend?" prompt to disrupt self-critical distortions.

## Step 6: Name progress instead of perfection

Once a week, add a brief entry titled **"Not perfect, but progress."** List 3 things that were not all or nothing:

- "I did some of my workout, not all of it."
- "I answered two emails instead of the whole inbox."
- "I ate one nourishing meal even though the rest of the day was chaotic."

This trains your brain to notice partial success, which gradually weakens the grip of perfectionism.

## Step 7: A gentle script for extreme days

On days when everything feels "ruined," you can use this simple script:

- "Right now my mind is telling me the story that…"
- "The feelings behind that story are…"
- "If I zoom out a little, one other way to describe today is…"

You do not have to believe the new description fully. You are simply giving your brain one more path to practice.
