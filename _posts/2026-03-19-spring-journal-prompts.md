There is something almost nervous about spring. After months of contraction and quiet, the world starts up again — louder, greener, more insistent — before you have necessarily finished the inside work that winter asked for. You may not feel as ready as the daffodils seem to be.

That tension is one of the things the spring journal is for.

These prompts are not about productivity, goal-setting, or treating spring as a second New Year. They are about the actual texture of this season: the light coming back, the body unclenching slightly, the things that wintered over in you beginning to stir. And for some of them, the tiredness of having made it through another dark season that deserves acknowledgement before you move on.

### 20 Spring Journal Prompts

**Arriving in the season**
1. *"What I notice in my body as the light changes is..."*
2. *"After the winter I just had, arriving into spring feels like..."*
3. *"Something I buried or set aside during the cold months that I am ready to pick up again is..."*
4. *"A part of myself that contracted in winter that I want to gently invite back out is..."*

**What winter taught you**
5. *"Something I learned about myself in the last few dark months is..."*
6. *"What I needed that I did not have enough of this winter was..."*
7. *"A way I took care of myself this winter that I want to carry forward is..."*
8. *"Something I am leaving in the winter that I no longer need to carry is..."*

**What is beginning**
9. *"A small thing I want to begin in the next few weeks — not a resolution, just a beginning — is..."*
10. *"An energy or quality that feels like it is returning to me this season is..."*
11. *"Something I was not ready for last spring that I might be ready for now is..."*
12. *"A project, relationship, or part of my life that is asking for more attention this spring is..."*

**On hope and the tender parts**
13. *"Something I am allowing myself to hope for this year — tentatively, without pressure — is..."*
14. *"A way this spring feels different from last spring is..."*
15. *"Something that feels tender or new right now, like a thing that has not been tested yet, is..."*

**For the senses and the present**
16. *"Three specific things about this season that I want to actually notice and remember are..."*
17. *"A pleasure I have been postponing that spring makes more available is..."*
18. *"What 'being outside more' would actually look like for me, realistically, in this season is..."*

**Closing the season's opening**
19. *"If I could give myself one permission for the whole of spring, it would be..."*
20. *"What I most want to feel by the time summer arrives is..."*

### On Spring and New Beginnings Pressure

Spring carries almost as much performance pressure as the New Year. The sense that you should be emerging, thriving, feeling lighter and more energetic by virtue of the calendar can be genuinely alienating if you are still tired, still depressed, still in the middle of something difficult.

If that is where you are: spring is not rushing you. The season will come and go regardless of whether you use it to transform yourself. Simply arriving in it, and noticing what is here, is enough.

The [winter journal prompts](/winter-journal-prompts/) post has prompts specifically for the slower, more interior side of the year. And if spring is difficult for particular mental health reasons — including spring-onset anxiety or mood changes related to light sensitivity — the [journaling for anxiety](/journaling-for-anxiety/) and [journaling for depression](/journaling-for-depression/) posts may be more useful starting points.

### A Note on Seasonal Journaling

Spring is the fourth seasonal post on this site — completing the cycle alongside [summer](/summer-journal-prompts/), [autumn](/autumn-journal-prompts/), and [winter](/winter-journal-prompts/). If you enjoy returning to the journal at seasonal transitions, each post has its own distinct tone: summer for vitality and the questions that long days make room for, autumn for letting go and the harvest of the year, winter for interiority and rest.

There is something grounding about checking in with yourself at each turning. You do not need to be a person who journals every day to find value in stopping four times a year to ask: *where am I, what has changed, and what do I want to bring forward?*
