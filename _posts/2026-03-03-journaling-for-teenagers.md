If you are a teenager reading this: this page is for you first.

You are navigating something genuinely difficult. Adolescence is not just a phase you pass through, it is a period of massive neurological change, identity formation, social pressure, and emotional intensity that is often dismissed by the adults around you as drama or hormones. Much of what you feel is real, significant, and worth taking seriously.

Journaling is one way to take your own inner life seriously. You do not need to be a writer. You do not need to be consistent. You do not need to produce anything anyone else would find interesting. You just need a place where you are allowed to be honest without performing anything for anyone.

---

### Why Journaling Can Help During Adolescence

Adolescence is when the brain's prefrontal cortex, the part responsible for emotional regulation, long-term thinking, and impulse control, is still developing. This is not a flaw; it is why teenagers experience emotions as more intense and immediate than adults tend to. The feelings are not exaggerated. They are genuinely big.

Writing is one of the few tools that engages the prefrontal cortex directly. When you put a difficult feeling into words, you shift the processing from pure emotional reaction toward something slightly more reflective. You don't feel the emotion less, but you can often understand it better and respond to it more on your own terms.

Research with adolescents has found that expressive journaling can reduce anxiety, improve self-esteem, and help young people process social stress more effectively. It is also one of the few mental health tools that does not require talking to anyone, remembering to take something, or spending money.

---

### On Privacy

Your journal is private. Full stop.

If you are worried about someone reading it, a parent, sibling, or flatmate, that is a legitimate concern. Some options: a notebook with a lock, a journal app with a password (Day One, Diarium, and Rosarium all have password protection), a notes app where the file title looks boring, or keeping it somewhere genuinely private.

If you are a parent or carer reading this: please do not read your teenager's journal. A private journal is only useful if it is actually private. The whole point is that it is a space without audience, without judgement, and without consequences. Reading it, even with benevolent intentions, destroys that entirely, and is likely to damage trust rather than protect your young person. The note for parents at the end of this post addresses what to do if you have genuine concerns about safety.

### Getting Started

The single most common barrier for teenagers and journaling is not having time or not knowing what to write. Both are real, and both have simple solutions.

**Time**: Five minutes while waiting for something, before bed, or during any ten-minute window that would otherwise be phone scrolling. You do not need a full hour and a nice candle. You need a pen and a few minutes.

**Not knowing what to write**: Start with a sentence. *"Today I feel [word]."* Or *"The thing that is on my mind right now is..."* Or just describe what you can see from where you are sitting. The page does not care how you start.

### Journal Prompts Specifically for Teenagers

**About your daily experience**
1. *"The best part of today was... and the worst part was... and the thing I didn't talk to anyone about was..."*
2. *"Something that happened this week that I'm still thinking about is..."*
3. *"Something I said today that wasn't quite true to how I actually felt is..."*

**About identity and who you are**
4. *"Some things about who I'm becoming that I actually like are..."*
5. *"A version of myself that I perform for other people, versus how I actually feel inside, is..."*
6. *"Something I believe that most people in my life don't know about me is..."*
7. *"What I actually want for myself in the next few years, underneath the 'should' answers, is..."*

**About relationships and social life**
8. *"The person I feel most like myself around is... and why is..."*
9. *"A friendship or relationship that is making me feel worse about myself, and what I want to do about it, is..."*
10. *"Something I wish someone in my life understood about me is..."*

**For hard feelings**
11. *"Right now I'm feeling [word]. What it feels like in my body is... What I think is causing it is... What might help is..."*
12. *"Something I've been angry about that I haven't said out loud is..."*
13. *"A fear I have that I haven't told anyone is..."*

**For lighter days**
14. *"Something that genuinely made me happy recently, even briefly, was..."*
15. *"A thing I'm looking forward to, even if it's small, is..."*

### A Note for Parents and Carers

The most useful thing an adult can do to support a teenager's journaling practice is to leave it entirely alone while making it clear that the teenager has other options if they want to talk.

If you have genuine safety concerns, you believe your young person is in danger of harming themselves or others, that requires a different response than reading their journal. Direct conversation, professional support (via their GP, school counsellor, or a service like Childline in the UK), and keeping communication as open as possible are all more appropriate responses than covert reading.

If your teenager tells you they journal, the supportive response is something like: *"That's great. It's completely yours."* Not: *"Oh great, can I read it?"*

Supporting your teenager's inner life means respecting that it is theirs.
