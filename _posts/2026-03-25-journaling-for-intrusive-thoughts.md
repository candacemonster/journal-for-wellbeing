Intrusive thoughts are thoughts that arrive unbidden and feel deeply wrong. They tend to be the opposite of what you want, believe, or value — unwanted images of harm, fears about your own nature, obsessive doubts, thoughts that feel contaminating simply by being thought.

What makes intrusive thoughts so distressing is that the mind treats them as significant. If the thought keeps returning, it must mean something. If you had that image, it must say something about who you are.

Neither of those conclusions is correct. Intrusive thoughts are experienced by the vast majority of people. Research by psychologists including Stanley Rachman and Adam Radomsky found that intrusive unwanted thoughts are nearly universal — the difference between ordinary intrusive thoughts and OCD is not the content of the thought but the significance attributed to it and the behaviours used to manage it.

This matters for journaling because the wrong kind of journaling can make intrusive thoughts significantly worse.

---

### The Trap: Reassurance-Seeking in Writing

The most important thing to understand before journaling about intrusive thoughts is this: **writing designed to resolve the uncertainty does not help**. If you are writing *"but do I actually believe this thought? am I actually the kind of person who would do this? let me reason through whether this thought means something bad about me..."* you are engaging in a form of reassurance-seeking.

Reassurance-seeking — whether done by asking others or by extensive self-analysis — maintains and amplifies intrusive thoughts rather than reducing them. It reinforces the message that the thought requires resolution. The thought responds by returning more urgently.

This is particularly important for OCD, where journaling can easily become a compulsion: a ritual analysis of the thought that provides temporary relief but increases the overall frequency and distress of the thought pattern.

If you are working with a therapist using ERP (Exposure and Response Prevention) for OCD, follow their guidance on journaling rather than this post's, as the clinical context may change what is appropriate.

---

### What Helpful Writing Looks Like Instead

**Observation without engagement**
Rather than engaging with the content of the intrusive thought (analysing whether it is true, what it means, what you would actually do), you can write *about* having the thought from a slight distance.

*"I noticed the thought that [x] again today. It showed up while I was [context]. My body's response was [description]. I observed it and then it passed."*

This is a defusion technique drawn from Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT). You are treating the thought as an observable event rather than a claim requiring response.

**Writing about the distress, not the thought**
You can write about how it feels to have intrusive thoughts without writing about the content of those thoughts.

*"It has been a hard day for intrusive thoughts. The anxiety peaked around mid-afternoon. What helped was [x]. What I notice I am feeling underneath the intrusive thoughts is [fear / exhaustion / shame]. What I actually need is [compassion / rest / distraction]."*

**Compassionate observation**
Many people feel profound shame about their intrusive thoughts, partly because the content is so contrary to their values. Writing with explicit self-compassion — acknowledging that having intrusive thoughts does not make you a bad person, and that what the thoughts feel like says nothing about what you actually believe or would do — can reduce the shame layer that often amplifies distress.

---

### Prompts for Intrusive Thoughts (Written Carefully)

1. *"What I need right now — not to resolve the thought, but to take care of the person having it — is..."*
2. *"The emotion underneath the intrusive thoughts today, if I sit with it without engaging the thought's content, is..."*
3. *"Something I know to be true about who I am that is incompatible with what the thought suggests is..."*
4. *"A way I would speak to a friend who told me they were having the same type of thoughts I am having is..."*
5. *"One thing that genuinely helped when the thoughts were loudest was..."*

### When to Seek Professional Support

If intrusive thoughts are significantly disruptive to your daily life, are accompanied by compulsive behaviours or avoidances, or if you are struggling to distinguish between intrusive thoughts and genuine intentions or plans, please do seek professional support. OCD is highly treatable with the right therapeutic approach (ERP in particular has the strongest evidence base), and a good therapist can help you develop a journaling practice that supports rather than interferes with treatment.

The journal is one tool. For intrusive thoughts at clinical levels, it works best as part of a larger, supported approach.
