There is something in autumn that asks us to slow down before we are ready. The light shifts. The temperature drops by increments. The pace that summer sustains starts to feel costly. And underneath all of it, a quieter question begins to surface: what from this year needs to be set down before the year ends?

Autumn has always been a natural season for reflection — a harvest of experience, a preparation for winter, a gentle reckoning with what has grown and what has not. Journaling in autumn can follow that rhythm without forcing it.

These prompts are for the slow, honest kind of reflection. Not productivity audits. Not year-in-review checklists. Just an invitation to sit with the season and ask some of the gentler questions.

---

### On the Particular Quality of Autumn Writing

Autumn invites a specific kind of introspection that differs from other seasons. Summer's outward energy — plans, movement, sunlight — recedes. The external world becomes quieter in a way that makes the internal world more audible.

Many people notice that their emotional landscape shifts in autumn: old feelings resurface, a general quietening sets in, and there is both melancholy and a kind of comfort in the shortening days. Rather than pushing past this or filling it with busyness, the journal can be a place to actually inhabit it.

These prompts honour that texture.

---

### 20 Autumn Journal Prompts

**For the seasonal shift itself**
1. *"What does the change in light or temperature feel like in my body this week?"*
2. *"Something I am ready to let go of as summer ends is..."*
3. *"A feeling that has arrived with autumn that I have not fully named yet is..."*
4. *"If autumn were a message from the year, what it might be saying is..."*

**For the harvest of the year so far**
5. *"The thing I am most surprised to have started, survived, or finished this year is..."*
6. *"Something I planted at the beginning of the year that has grown in an unexpected direction is..."*
7. *"A lesson the year has been teaching me, slowly, is..."*
8. *"One way I am different this autumn compared to last autumn is..."*
9. *"Something I worked toward this year that I want to properly acknowledge, even if it is incomplete, is..."*

**For what needs to be let go**
10. *"A belief, habit, or expectation I have been carrying that is starting to feel too heavy is..."*
11. *"Something from this year that I want to mark, mourn, and gently release is..."*
12. *"A version of how things 'should have gone' that I am still arguing with is... and one sentence of making peace with how they actually went..."*

**For slowing down**
13. *"One way I could honestly slow down this autumn — not as a to-do, but as a genuine act of care — is..."*
14. *"The things I have been wanting that feel autumnal, cosy, quieter, more internal, are..."*
15. *"When I imagine a genuinely restful October or November, it includes..."*

**For what autumn feels like personally**
16. *"My relationship to autumn, emotionally and historically, has been..."*
17. *"A small, sensory detail of this specific autumn that I want to remember is..."*
18. *"Something that feels like home about this time of year is..."*

**For looking toward winter**
19. *"As the year moves into its final months, what I most want to carry forward is..."*
20. *"A word or phrase I want to hold as my quiet intention for the rest of the year is... and why that feels right is..."*

---

### On Using These Prompts

You do not have to answer all twenty. Pick one or two that pull at something. Return to the same prompt on different days and see what is different each time. You might find that some prompts work better in October, others in November, when the season has fully arrived.

Autumn journaling is at its best when it is unhurried. Set a kettle going, sit near a window, and let the pace of the writing match the pace of the season.

---

### If Autumn Is Hard for You

For some people, autumn brings Seasonal Affective Disorder (SAD), a recurrence of depression, or a general heaviness that has nothing to do with cosy aesthetics. If that is your experience, the language in these prompts is meant to be gentle and non-demanding. You are allowed to write "I don't know" in response to anything. You are allowed to use the journal to say "this season is difficult for me" before anything else.

The [journaling for depression](/journaling-for-depression/) post and the [journaling through fatigue](/journaling-through-fatigue/) post both have low-effort formats for very low-energy days.

The season will pass. The journal can be company while it does.
