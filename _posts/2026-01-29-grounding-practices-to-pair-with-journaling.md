Grounding practices help you settle into your body and the present moment, which can make journaling feel safer, more focused, and less overwhelming. These are not long meditations or complicated exercises, they are 1 to 3 minute practices you can use before or after writing to support your nervous system.

---

### Why Grounding Matters for Journaling

When you journal about difficult feelings or experiences, your nervous system can become activated, meaning you might feel more anxious, jittery, spacey, or numb. Grounding practices help you stay anchored while you explore what is hard, and they signal to your body that you are safe enough to process and then move on.

You can use grounding before you write to help you arrive and settle, or after you write to help you close the session and transition back into the rest of your day.

---

### 5-4-3-2-1 Sensory Awareness

Look around and slowly name:

- 5 things you can see
- 4 things you can touch or feel
- 3 things you can hear
- 2 things you can smell (or imagine smelling)
- 1 thing you can taste (or imagine tasting)

This practice gently pulls your attention out of your head and into the environment around you, which can be especially helpful if you are feeling anxious, flooded, or caught in rumination.

---

### Feet on the Floor

Sit with your feet flat on the ground. Notice the weight of your body being supported by the chair or floor. Press your feet down slightly and feel the pressure and contact. Take three slow breaths while keeping your attention on the feeling of being held by the surface beneath you.

This is one of the simplest and most effective grounding techniques, and it works well before journaling to help you settle, or after journaling if you feel unmoored.

---

### Hand on Heart, Hand on Belly

Place one hand on your chest or heart, and the other on your belly. Feel the warmth of your hands and the gentle rise and fall of your breath. You do not have to change your breathing, just notice it. Stay here for 5 to 10 breaths.

This practice can feel soothing and regulating, especially if you are feeling anxious or sad. The physical touch can remind your nervous system that you are here and that you are holding yourself with care.

---

### 4-7-8 Breathing (or Any Slow Breath Pattern)

Breathe in slowly through your nose for a count of 4, hold gently for a count of 7, and exhale slowly through your mouth for a count of 8. Repeat 3 to 5 times.

If those counts feel too long or uncomfortable, you can adjust to 3-5-6 or simply focus on making your exhale longer than your inhale. Slow, extended exhales activate the parasympathetic nervous system, which helps you calm down.

---

### Body Scan (Very Brief Version)

Starting at the top of your head, bring your attention down through your body in sections: head and face, neck and shoulders, chest and arms, belly and back, hips and legs, feet. As you notice each area, just observe any sensations, tension, or temperature without trying to fix or change anything.

This can take as little as 60 seconds if you move quickly, and it helps you reconnect with your physical self, which is especially useful if you tend to get stuck in your thoughts while journaling.

---

### Temperature Shift (Cold or Warm Sensation)

Hold an ice cube in your hand for 10 to 30 seconds, or splash cold water on your face. Alternatively, wrap your hands around a warm mug, hold a heating pad, or run warm water over your hands. The sharp sensory input can interrupt a spiral of distress and bring you back to the present.

Cold is especially effective if you are feeling flooded or panicky, while warmth can be soothing if you are feeling numb or shut down.

---

### Progressive Muscle Relaxation (Short Version)

Tense your shoulders up toward your ears and hold for 3 seconds, then release and let them drop. Notice the difference between tension and relaxation. You can do this with your hands (make fists, then release), your face (scrunch it up, then soften), or your whole body if you have a bit more time.

This practice helps you release physical tension that can build up while you are writing about hard things.

---

### Orienting to the Room

Without moving your head much, let your eyes slowly scan the room or space you are in. Notice colours, shapes, light, and objects. Let your gaze rest on something that feels neutral or pleasant. This practice can help you feel more located in space and less trapped in your inner experience.

---

### Gentle Movement or Stretching

Stand up and stretch your arms overhead, roll your shoulders back a few times, twist gently from side to side, or do a few slow neck rolls. Even 30 seconds of gentle movement can shift your state and help you feel more embodied.

If you have been sitting and writing for a while, movement before you close your journal can help you transition and release any held tension.

---

### Savoring a Small Comfort

Take a slow sip of tea, water, or coffee and really notice the temperature, taste, and sensation. Or step outside for a moment and feel the air on your skin. Or wrap yourself in a soft blanket and notice the texture and warmth. This practice of savoring a small, pleasant sensation can be grounding and kind.

---

### How to Choose Which Practice to Use

If you are feeling:

- **Anxious or activated:** Try 5-4-3-2-1, feet on floor, or cold water
- **Spacey or numb:** Try temperature shift, movement, or body scan
- **Sad or tender:** Try hand on heart, slow breathing, or savoring
- **Overwhelmed:** Try feet on floor, orienting to the room, or 4-7-8 breathing

You can also rotate through a few favorites and notice over time which ones feel most regulating for you. The goal is not to do grounding perfectly, it is to give your nervous system a small, kind signal that you are safe and present.
